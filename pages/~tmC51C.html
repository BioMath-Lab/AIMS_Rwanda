INTRODUCTION TO TSWALU KALAHARI RESERVE

Tswalu Kalahari Reserve (TKR) is a privately owned nature reserve situated in the Northern Cape province of South Africa (SA) between latitudes -27.5458 to -27.0781 and longitudes 22.1694 to 22.6064. Established in 1995, it covers an area of + 1 140 km2 and falls within the Eastern Kalahari Bushveld and Kalahari Duneveld Bioregions of SA (Rutherford et al. 2006).
The climate is hot (annual mean temperature = 20 Â°C) and dry (mean annual rainfall = + 360 mm) with highly variable summer rainfall between December and March (Wataru et al. 2018). Topography is highly variable, ranging from flat, low lying plains in the east (1019 MASL) to rugged mountains of the Korannaberg in the west (Bloukrans 1590.3 MASL). As a result, the underlying geology consists of predominantly unconsolidated sediments (87%), followed by metamorphic rocks (13%) and mixed sedimentary rocks (0.3%) (Hartmann and Moosdorf 2012). Soils are dominated by arenosols [Haplic Arenosols (45%); Luvic Arenosols (16%); Ferralic Arenosols (14%)], which are sandy-textured soils with little humus or clay found typically in deserts (Hengl et al. 2017). Smaller amounts of cambisols [Chromic Cambisols (17%)] and leptosols [Lithic Leptosols (7%)] with similarly shallow profile depths are also present (Hengl et al. 2017).
These underlying geology and soils give rise to five unique vegetation types described by Mucina and Rutherford (2006) as Gordonia Plains Shrubland (41%); Gordonia Duneveld (23%); Kathu Bushveld (15%); Koranna - Langeberg Mountain Bushveld (11%); and Olifantshoek Plains Thornveld (11%).
